               Operation
               The plunger of a fuel pump is actuated by a cam and roller tappet follower.  When the follower is
               on the heel of the cam the plunger is at the bottom of its stroke and the inlet port in the barrel is
               uncovered allowing fuel in the gallery which surrounds the barrel to flow into and fill that portion
               of the barrel above the plunger, Fig. 7-5 (a).  The plunger is a close fit within the barrel made of
               special steel or cast iron.  As the cam rotates the plunger rises and seals off the inlet ports, and at
               this point of the stroke the pumping action starts. Further upward movement of the plunger causes
               the fuel pressure to rise and expelled through the delivery valve to the injector.

               A helical groove extends from the top of the plunger partway down its cylindrical surface. Angular
               positioning of the plunger about its axis, will locate this groove in relation to the inlet port to give
               the  required  quantity  of  fuel  per  stroke-position  (b).  In  some  designs,  a  separate  spill  port  is
               provided  but  this  is  also  made  to  communicate  with  the  inlet  gallery.  Effective  delivery  of  fuel
               continues  until  the  plunger  reaches  position  (c),  when  the  lower  edge  of  the  control  helix  has
               uncovered the spill port in the barrel, thus allowing fuel to spill back to the inlet gallery of the pump
               which is at lower pressure than that inside of the barrel. This allows the delivery valve to close and
               stop further delivery and the unloaded volume of fuel is passed back to the inlet gallery.

               The plunger stroke is always constant but that part of it which is actually pumping is variable, by
               means of rotating the plunger to vary the stroke between port closure and port opening. Position (d)
               of Fig. 7-5 represents full load output whereas position (e) shows the idling condition. To stop the
               engine, the plunger is turned to a position so that the vertical slot is opposite the port and all the
               displaced fuel is spilled back to the inlet gallery, as shown in position (f).

               The amount of fuel delivered will vary in accordance with the distance separating the top of the
               plunger and the helix relative to the spill port. This angular position is controlled by a rack and
               pinion,  the  latter  forming  a  sleeve  having  an  axial  slot  or  slots  to  receive  a  projection  on  the
               plunger. The rack position therefore determines the quantity of fuel supplied. The plunger is a close
               fit within the barrels  to  ensure sealing at  high pressure.  However, some leakage is  necessary to
               provide lubrication for the relative movement.
