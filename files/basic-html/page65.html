5.10  Factors Influencing Engine Power Output
               The main factors that govern the indicated and brake power output of an engine are:


               5.10.1  Number and Dimension of Cylinders
               The indicated power output of an engine may be increased by increasing the bore and stroke; but
               such  increases  results  in  greater  inertia  forces  of  the  reciprocating  masses  (the  pistons  and
               connecting rods). Also large bore will lower the cooling effect on the piston and valves thereby
               reducing the volumetric efficiency and subsequently the indicated power output.

               An increase in piston stroke and the stroke to bore (S/D) ratio, keeping other engine dimensions
               unchanged leads to higher mean piston speed that reduces volumetric efficiency.  Therefore, it is
               more purposeful to increase the total piston displacement of an engine by adding more cylinders
               than by increasing cylinder dimensions.

               In  marine  diesel  engines  as  many  as  12  cylinders  can  be  used  in  in-line  engines  and  up  to  24
               cylinders  in  'V'  type  engines.  The  choice  of  the  number  of  cylinders  requires  consideration  for
               engine room space, inertia forces of the engine, frequency of engine load changes and the necessity
               for starting the engine from any position of its crankshaft. From the last requirement, it follows that
               diesel engine started by means of compressed air must have at least four cylinders if two-stroke and
               at least six cylinders if they are four-stroke engines.

               The higher the value of stroke to bore ratio (S/D), the greater the total height of the engine.
                        For:    low-speed engine S/D range is     -    1.4  -  2.2
                                 medium-speed engine                -    1.0  -   1.5
                                 high-speed  engine                     -    0.95(1.0)   -  1.3

               5.10.2  Number of Stroke per Cycle
               Engine  power  output  rises  with  a  reduction  in  the  number  of  stroke  required  per  cycle.  Thus,
               theoretically a two-stroke engine should be twice as powerful as four-stroke one with identical bore
               diameter, stroke, number of cylinders and speed. A two-stroke engine has a lower value of charge
               efficiency  due  to  inferior  scavenging  process  and  higher  air  temperature  at  the  beginning  of
               compression stroke. The loss of a portion of piston stroke occupied by scavenging ports results in a
               lower indicated thermal efficiency due to less perfect combustion. For these reasons, the indicated
               power output of a two-stroke engine is not twice that of a four- stroke. It is approximately 1.75 to
               1.85 times greater.

               5.10.3  Engine Speed Factor

               The brake power output of an engine should rise in direct proportion to engine speed (rev/min) and
               therefore to the mean piston speed. A rise in speed however results in increase in hydrodynamic
               resistance of the inlet and exhaust system that reduces the volumetric efficiency. Also increase in
               engine speed impairs the mixing process of fuel and air in the cylinder and thus affects the process
               of combustion, and reduces the thermal efficiency and air-fuel ratio.
